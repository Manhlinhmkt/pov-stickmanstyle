## WORLD CONTEXT

- **Domain:** MILITARY
- **Era:** MODERN_CONTEMPORARY
- **Veil Strictness:** None (GENERIC_ARCHETYPE - no veil enforcement needed)

### Key Facts Used

1. US Air Force RPA pilots train for approximately 1 year through UPT then specialized RPA pipeline before qualifying on MQ-9 Reaper - used in PHASE_EARLY (training arc)
2. Operators work rotating shifts (days, swings, mids) from Ground Control Stations at Creech AFB, Nevada - used in PHASE_CONFLICT (daily routine)
3. Before a strike, operators conduct Pattern of Life surveillance - watching a target's daily routines, family interactions, and movements for weeks or months - used in PHASE_CONFLICT (core concept)
4. After a strike, operators must stay on target for Battle Damage Assessment - watching the aftermath in high-definition - used in PHASE_CRISIS (aftermath)
5. Operators experience psychological whiplash - transitioning from a lethal combat mission to suburban family life in the same day - used in PHASE_CONFLICT and PHASE_CRISIS (emotional core)
6. PTSD rates among drone operators are comparable to those of combat-deployed personnel, despite never being physically in danger - used in PHASE_RESOLUTION (diagnosis)
7. The RPA community has historically faced stigma from the broader fighter-pilot culture - being told their experience does not count - used in PHASE_CONFLICT (identity conflict) and PHASE_RESOLUTION (invalidation)
8. A single Reaper orbit provides 24-hour persistent surveillance coverage, requiring crews to rotate continuously - used in PHASE_CONFLICT (operational tempo)

### Key Locations

- **LOC_GROUND_CONTROL_STATION** - A windowless, air-conditioned trailer housing multiple screens, joystick, and throttle - where the operator flies and kills from
- **LOC_CREECH_AFB** - Remote desert base 45 miles northwest of Las Vegas - the epicenter of US drone operations
- **LOC_SUBURBAN_HOME** - The operator's house in a Las Vegas suburb - the place they return to after each shift
- **LOC_TARGET_ZONE** - A village, compound, or road thousands of miles away - seen only through a screen but inhabiting the operator's mind

### Key Tensions

- **DUTY_VS_SELF** - You were trained to follow orders. The order is to kill. Your conscience says otherwise. But you press the button anyway - because that is what you trained for.
- **POWER_VS_ISOLATION** - You have the power to end lives from 10,000 miles away. But no one around you - not your wife, not your friends, not even other pilots - understands what that feels like.
- **NORMAL_DESIRE_VS_IMPOSSIBLE_REALITY** - You want to be a normal parent, a normal spouse. But you carry images behind your eyes that make normal impossible.
- **PROTECTION_VS_CONTROL** - You are told you protect your country. But what you do feels less like protection and more like control from above.

## CONFIRMED OUTLINE

### HOOK
- Screen glowing, hand on joystick, aerial view of a village from above
- "You have never set foot here. But you know every person by name."

### PHASE_EARLY: Recruitment + Training
- Sign up for Air Force, dream of flying fighter jets
- Assigned to RPA - "not a real aircraft"
- 1 year training pipeline: UPT to RPA to MQ-9 Reaper
- First day at Creech AFB - Nevada desert, windowless trailer
- "You are a pilot. But you never leave the ground."

### PHASE_CONFLICT: Pattern of Life
- Rotating shifts: day/swing/mid - the screen never turns off
- First mission: surveillance on a man - callsign JACKPOT - for 6 continuous weeks
- You watch him eat breakfast. Watch him take his child to school. Watch him pray at dawn.
- You know his sleep schedule. You know his Friday dinner recipe. You know his youngest plays in the front yard.
- He does not know you exist. You know everything about him.
- "Pattern of Life" - the official term. You document a man's life to determine when to end it.
- Come home after shift at 6 PM - wife asks "how was your day?" - "fine"
- Son asks what you do for work. You say: "Dad works with computers."
- Fellow fighter pilots call you "chair force" - your job "does not count as combat"
- But every morning you drive 45 minutes from suburban Las Vegas to the base, and every evening you drive back - past schools, grocery stores, parks - as if nothing happened
- "Psychological whiplash" - the military doctor will call it that later. You do not know the name yet. You just know you talk less and less.

### PHASE_CRISIS: The Strike Order
- Week 7. JACKPOT is sitting in his yard with his family. His wife is pouring tea. The little one is running around the table.
- Order comes through the headset: "Strike authorized. Cleared hot."
- Sensor operator beside you goes silent. You see the child sit down next to his father.
- You ask again: "Confirm - civilians in compound." Response: "Acknowledged. Cleared hot. Execute."
- Hand on the trigger. 3 seconds. You press.
- Hellfire drops. 8 seconds of flight. Screen flashes white then returns.
- Battle Damage Assessment - you are required to stay and watch. HD. Zoom in.
- The yard has no table. No chairs. No one sitting anymore.
- Sensor operator removes his headset, walks outside the trailer for 5 minutes. No one says anything.
- Shift ends. You drive home. Stop at McDonald's for a Happy Meal for your daughter.
- Daughter runs to hug your legs at the door. Same age as the child on the screen.
- Wife asks: "How was your day?" You say: "Fine."
- That night you cannot sleep. Every time you close your eyes, you see the yard.

### PHASE_RESOLUTION: The Cost of Safety
- Days after, you still show up. Still sit in the chair. Still turn on the screen. But your hands shake more.
- Strike two. Three. Seven. You stop counting.
- You start drinking after shifts. Then before. Then during.
- Wife says: "I do not recognize you anymore." You do not either.
- See the military doctor. They ask: "Have you experienced combat?" You do not know how to answer. You have never been shot at. Never ran. Never feared for your life.
- But you have killed. Many times. From a swivel chair.
- PTSD diagnosed - but the file reads "non-combat related stress." Infantry veterans from Afghanistan look at you like a fraud.
- Leave the military. Find civilian work. IT support for an insurance company.
- Sit in front of a screen 8 hours a day. Identical. But no one dies.
- Your hands still remember the joystick. Sometimes you zoom into Google Maps - look down at villages - then wonder why you do it.
- Wife is gone. Daughter calls once a week. You want to tell her what you did. But you never do.
- "You were safe. You were always safe. That is the worst part."

### CALLBACK_CLOSE
- Another screen. This time a laptop, your own home, a normal job
- You look down at the keyboard. Identical to before. Just missing one button.
- "If you ever wondered what it feels like to kill from 10,000 miles away - now you know."
